Figure It Out

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: When faced with academic challenges in learning either a new concept or subject matter, what strategies do you use to learn the new material?

The first thing I do when I hit something I genuinely do not understand is just sit with it for a minute. Not in a frustrated way. More like I am trying to figure out exactly what part I am missing. Because there is a real difference between not understanding something and not understanding one specific piece of it. If I can locate that one piece, I can usually work my way through the rest.

I learned that approach from coding. When I first started building websites, I would run into errors that looked completely foreign to me. My instinct early on was to just search for the whole thing and copy whatever came up. But I noticed that never really stuck. I would fix the problem without understanding why the fix worked, so the next time something similar came up I was right back at square one.

So I changed how I approached it. Instead of searching for the answer, I started searching for the concept underneath the problem. I would find someone who explained it visually, watch how they walked through it, and then go back to my own work and try to apply it myself. Not copy it. Actually rebuild it from scratch based on what I had just seen. That is when things started clicking in a way they had not before.

I use that same process in school. When something in my networking or cybersecurity coursework at FTCC did not land the first time, I did not just reread the same notes. I went and found a different explanation, a different angle on the same concept. Sometimes that was a video. Sometimes a forum. Sometimes I just explained out loud what I thought I knew and listened for where I got stuck.

Teaching other people is honestly the strategy that works best for me. When I started helping students in Code Forward, I realized pretty quickly that the moments when I struggled to explain something clearly were exactly the moments that showed me I had not fully learned it yet. There is no better test of your understanding than trying to break something down for someone who has never seen it before.

I also ask for help without making it a whole thing. I think some students treat asking a question like an admission of failure. I just do not see it that way. If a teacher or a peer can save me an hour of going in circles, I am going to ask. But I do not ask before I have tried. I want to come to that conversation with at least a theory about what is going wrong, because that is usually what gets me to a real answer instead of just a quick fix I will not remember.

The through line in all of it is that I treat not knowing something as a problem worth solving, not a reason to feel behind. I have been a self-taught learner for most of my technical skills, which means I have had a lot of practice at figuring things out with no guaranteed path forward. That is a muscle, and it has gotten stronger every year.
