Scholarship Essays

Wisdom M. Johnson  |  Pine Forest High School  |  Fayetteville, NC

Figure It Out

Prompt: When faced with academic challenges in learning either a new concept or subject matter, what strategies do you use to learn the new material?

Early on when I was learning to code, I had a habit that was quietly killing my progress. Every time I hit an error I didn't understand, I'd search for the answer, copy whatever came up, and move on. The problem would disappear. But I hadn't actually learned anything,I'd just patched it. And every time something similar came up, I was starting from zero again.

At some point I realized the issue wasn't the errors. It was that I was solving the symptom instead of understanding the cause. So I changed one thing: instead of searching for the fix, I started searching for the concept underneath it. Then I'd close the tab and try to rebuild the solution from scratch on my own. That one shift changed everything. When you understand why something works, it actually stays with you.

That became my approach to learning in general. When something in my networking coursework at FTCC didn't click, I didn't reread the same notes louder. I found a different explanation,a different angle on the same idea,until one of them matched how my brain was already thinking about it. The material usually isn't the problem. The explanation is. And there's almost always another one out there.

The deeper lesson is that not knowing something is just a problem to solve, not a reason to feel behind. I've been self-teaching technical skills since ninth grade, which means I've had a lot of practice sitting with confusion and working through it anyway. That's the only real strategy,stay in it long enough to find the concept that makes everything else make sense.

Representation Is a Resource

Prompt: How has your ethnicity played a role in shaping your STEM education and goals?

Growing up as a Black young man interested in technology, one of the first things I noticed was how few people who looked like me showed up in the spaces I was trying to enter. Not in the programs, not in the courses, not in the images of who gets to be called an engineer. That absence does something to you. It makes you wonder, quietly, whether those spaces were actually built for you.

What it did to me specifically was make me intentional in a way I don't think I would have been otherwise. I wasn't operating inside a system that was going to hand me opportunities, so I had to go find them. That's why I joined Upward Bound. That's why I enrolled in the Academy of Information Technology. That's why I took dual enrollment courses at FTCC while still in high school. Not because someone mapped that path out for me,because I understood early that the gap between where I was and where I wanted to be wasn't going to close by waiting.

Upward Bound is the one I keep coming back to. I'm a first-generation college student. My parents hadn't been through this process, which meant I was building the path at the same time I was walking it,figuring out financial aid, application timelines, what a weighted GPA actually signals, all of it in real time. Upward Bound didn't just give me college prep. It gave me the belief that I was someone this process was designed for, even when nothing around me was confirming that. That's not a small thing. For a lot of first-generation students, that belief is the whole difference.

Choosing NC A&T for Mechanical Engineering is the natural extension of all of it. NC A&T is the top producer of Black engineers in the country. Going there means walking into a room where my identity and my ambitions don't have to compete with each other. My background is not a footnote to my STEM story. It's the reason the story has the shape it does.

Already Started

Prompt: How do you plan to use your STEM degree to give back to the community?

The honest answer is that I've been thinking about this question since before I knew I was thinking about it. Growing up in Fayetteville, watching my mom counsel veterans at the VA and my dad work in marketing, I learned early that the work you do either reaches people or it doesn't,and that gap is usually a choice someone made upstream.

Upward Bound was the clearest example of that in my own life. Someone decided to build a program for first-generation students, fund it, staff it with people who actually showed up, and put it in front of kids who had no other roadmap. That program changed my trajectory. Not because it handed me anything, but because it made the path visible when I couldn't see it on my own. That's the thing I want to replicate.

When I finish my Mechanical Engineering degree at NC A&T, I want to work on infrastructure and design problems in communities where investment has historically been thin. Not as charity,as real engineering. The communities most underserved by bad infrastructure are also the ones with the least leverage to demand better, and that's exactly where the work should go. Design decisions that look neutral on paper end up benefiting some people and skipping others entirely. I want to be the engineer who catches that before it ships.

And I want to mentor,specifically the students in Fayetteville who are right where I was a few years ago, talented and curious but not sure if this path is actually open to them. I want to be the person who makes that answer clearly yes. The same way Upward Bound did it for me, I want to do it for them. That's not a future plan. That's the whole point.
