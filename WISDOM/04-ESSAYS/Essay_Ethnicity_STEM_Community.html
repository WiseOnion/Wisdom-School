Representation Is a Resource

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: How has your ethnicity played a role in shaping your STEM education and goals? How do you plan to use your STEM degree to give back to the community?

Growing up as a Black young man who was genuinely interested in technology, one of the first things I noticed was how few people who looked like me showed up in the spaces I was trying to enter. Not in the programs, not in the courses, not in the images of who gets to be called an engineer or a developer. And that absence does something to you, whether you are consciously aware of it or not. It makes you wonder, quietly, whether those spaces were actually built for you.

But something shifted the more I pushed through and built real skills. I started to see that absence differently. Not as a sign that I did not belong, but as a reason that I needed to be there. Representation is not just about feeling seen. It is genuinely a resource. When a young person sees someone who shares their background doing something, it expands what they believe is possible for themselves. I know that because it happened to me, and because I watched it happen to students I taught.

My ethnicity shaped my STEM path in a very specific way. It made me intentional. I could not afford to be passive about my learning, because I was not operating inside a system that was automatically going to put opportunities in front of me. I had to seek things out. Upward Bound, the Academy of Information Technology, dual enrollment at FTCC, teaching myself to code before any class covered it. I learned early that the gap between where I was and where I wanted to be was not going to close by waiting around for it.

Choosing NC A&T for mechanical engineering was not random. NC A&T is the top producer of Black engineers in the country. Walking into that environment as a Black man who wants to build things means being in a space that was designed with people like me in mind, surrounded by peers and professors who understand what it takes to get there and push you to go further.

As for giving back, I am honestly already doing a version of it. Through Code Forward, I teach young people in my community how to use technology to build real things. Through WisdomLayers, I build tools for small businesses in Fayetteville that cannot afford to be left behind digitally. Those are not separate from my STEM path. They are the whole point of it.

When I have my engineering degree, I want to apply that same energy at a much larger scale. I want to work on infrastructure and design problems that affect communities where investment has historically been thin. I want to mentor younger students who are right where I was, trying to figure out if this path is really open to them. And I want to help build the kind of institutions that create generational opportunity in communities like the one I came from.

My background is not a footnote to my STEM story. It is the reason the story has the shape it does. And giving back is not something I plan to do once I have made it. It is something I am building into the work right now.
