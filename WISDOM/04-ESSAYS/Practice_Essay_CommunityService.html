What Fayetteville Gave Me, and What I Gave Back

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: How have you served your community?

Fayetteville is a military city. People come and go quickly, and the communities that stay together are the ones that actively choose to show up for each other. I grew up watching that kind of commitment at St. Matthews Church, where the same faces showed up every single week, not because anyone required it, but because those people understood that showing up is itself a form of service. I started volunteering in childcare there when I was twelve. I am still doing it. Five years later, three hours a week, every week.

That consistency is something I think about a lot. Anyone can volunteer once for a resume. Sustained service is different. It requires that you actually care about the place and the people, not just the line item. The children I work with at St. Matthews see the same face every week, and there is something in that reliability that matters more than the total hours. My mother is a counselor at the VA Medical Hospital. My father works in marketing. Neither of them ever sat me down and explained that showing up consistently is a form of integrity. But they both lived it, and I learned it from watching them.

My time at the North Regional Library pushed that commitment in a different direction. I logged over 50 hours in a single year working directly with patrons, helping people find resources, assisting with computer use, and supporting programs. What I did not expect was how much it would teach me. The library serves everyone: students doing homework, adults filing for benefits online, seniors learning how to use email for the first time. It is one of the few genuinely public spaces left, and volunteering there forced me to communicate across every kind of difference you can imagine, age, background, skill level, patience. That is a skill I carry into every other space I work in.

Code Forward was a different kind of service entirely. I was not just giving time. I was giving knowledge. I led coding sessions for students who had never written a single line of code, teaching HTML, CSS, and the basic logic of how a webpage comes together. The hardest part of teaching is not knowing your material. It is figuring out which explanation will actually land for a specific person in a specific moment. I learned to listen before I explained, to ask questions before I demonstrated, and to let students work through the confusion instead of rushing past it. That is not just good teaching. It is a form of respect.

Through the 100 Black Men of Fayetteville, I saw mentorship from a different angle, what it means to be guided by people who came from your same community, went further, and chose to come back and show the way. I want to be part of that cycle. Going to NC A&T as a Mechanical Engineering student is not just about where I am going. It is about what I plan to come back and build once I get there. The community that shaped me is the same one I intend to serve. I have not forgotten that, and I do not plan to.
