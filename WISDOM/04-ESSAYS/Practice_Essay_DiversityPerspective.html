What I Bring to the Room

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: How will you contribute to campus diversity and community?

I grew up in Fayetteville, North Carolina, which is a city built around Fort Liberty. The population turns over every two or three years, and the community that stays is the one that chose to build roots despite all of that instability. Growing up there, you learn pretty quickly that people carry very different versions of the same country in their heads, and that learning to navigate those differences is not optional. It is just the daily reality.

I am a first-generation college student. My parents did not attend college before earning their degrees, and the roadmap I followed to get here was not handed to me. I built it by joining TRIO Upward Bound, finding mentors, asking questions I sometimes felt embarrassed to ask, and learning that the most important skill in any new environment is knowing how to figure out what you do not yet know. That is not a background I am apologetic about. It is a perspective I plan to bring into every collaboration, every project team, and every conversation I have in college.

Choosing NC A&T was a deliberate decision. HBCUs were built on the premise that Black excellence is not an exception, it is the expectation. Going there as a Black man who wants to study Mechanical Engineering means choosing an environment where my identity and my ambitions do not have to be in tension. I am not arriving to represent something. I am arriving to learn, to build, and to contribute to a community that has been producing engineers and innovators for over a century.

The diversity I bring is not only demographic. It is experiential. I founded a web development company from my bedroom in Fayetteville. I taught coding to students who had never written a line of it before. I have been playing piano for four years, training in Ninjutsu for three, and volunteering consistently at my church and local library since middle school. My perspective was shaped by a military city, a first-generation household, a public school technical academy, and the particular kind of drive that comes from building things before you are certain you know how.

At NC A&T I want to be someone who makes engineering feel accessible in a real way, not just as an idea but as something people around me believe they can do. I want to get involved in programs that bring technical skills to students who did not grow up with access to them. I want to push my project teams to think about who we are designing for and whether our default assumptions actually hold. I want to prove, day after day, that where I came from is not a disadvantage. It is a lens that makes me a more thorough engineer and a more useful teammate.

Diversity is not something you add to a campus. It is what happens when people with genuinely different experiences decide to build something together. I am bringing mine.
