Built to Build

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: Why do you want to study engineering?

I did not really decide to become an engineer. I just started noticing that I already thought like one.

The first time I built a website for a real client, not a class project, not a practice exercise, but an actual person who needed something made and was counting on me to deliver it, I ran into a problem I had never seen before. The layout broke on mobile. The images stacked wrong. The navigation disappeared on smaller screens. I could have sent it back and said I would fix it later. Instead I just sat with it. I broke the problem into pieces, worked through each one until I understood what was causing it, and kept going until every screen size rendered exactly right. When it finally worked, something clicked that no assignment had ever produced. That feeling of diagnosing why something fails and designing a fix that actually holds, that is what I want to spend my career doing.

Engineering is not about having all the answers. It is about having a process for finding them. In the Academy of Information Technology at Pine Forest, I learned how systems fail and how they are designed not to. In Entrepreneurship I and II, I learned how to frame a problem before jumping to a solution. At FTCC, my dual enrollment courses in networking and cybersecurity showed me how every layer of a system depends on every other layer. These were not just classes. They were frameworks for thinking. And I kept running into the same pattern across all of them: understand the system, find the weak point, redesign it.

When I founded WisdomLayers, I was not thinking about it as a business at first. I was thinking about a design problem. People in my community needed websites but could not afford agencies. Students at my school did not realize web development was something they could actually do. I built the company to address both of those things at once, serve real clients at real prices and prove that a teenager from Fayetteville, NC can build something worth paying for. Code Forward gave me another angle on the same problem: how do you teach someone to think in systems when they have never written a single line of code? You break it down. You find the entry point. You build from there.

Mechanical Engineering at North Carolina A&T is where I want to take those instincts next. The problems are bigger, thermal systems, structural loads, material tolerances, but the thinking is the same. I want to work on physical systems the same way I have worked on digital ones, not just building what already exists, but finding where the design can actually be better. NC A&T's program and its E-Scholars community are exactly the kind of environment where that kind of thinking gets developed and pushed further.

Honestly, I have been preparing for this longer than I realized. Every website I built, every bug I solved, every student I helped debug their first line of code, those were not just extracurriculars. They were practice. Engineering is the field where that kind of thinking is not just welcomed. It is required.
