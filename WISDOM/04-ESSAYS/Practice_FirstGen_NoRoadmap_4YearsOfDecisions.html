No Roadmap

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: Describe a challenge you have overcome.

The challenge I have been navigating is not a single moment. It is a condition. And working through it has taken four years of consistent decisions, not one big breakthrough.

I am a first-generation college student. Neither of my parents attended college before earning their degrees, and the process of getting here, the timelines, the financial aid system, the difference between early decision and regular decision, the way class rank actually works, none of that was something I inherited. I did not grow up in a household where college application season was familiar, or where someone could sit down and walk me through what a weighted GPA signals to an admissions committee. I had to learn all of it in real time, often from people who assumed I already knew.

In ninth grade I joined TRIO Upward Bound because I understood even then that I needed a structure that could teach me what I did not yet know how to ask about. That program gave me mentors, college visits, and a community of students who were navigating the exact same gap. It also helped me get comfortable with the fact that financial need was not something to minimize or explain away. It was a real part of my story, and I learned to speak to it clearly and without apology.

What made the challenge harder was maintaining academic performance while carrying a schedule that looked nothing like what most high-achieving students describe. I was not just studying. I was volunteering at the library for over 50 hours, running childcare at church, training in martial arts, practicing piano, learning web development on my own, and eventually running a business. I could not afford to let any one piece collapse, so I learned to manage my time and energy the same way I manage code: in layers, with structure, and with a clear sense of what depends on what.

The result is a 4.000 weighted GPA and a class rank of 51 out of 342. Those numbers matter to me not because they look impressive on paper, but because I know what it took to earn them in the conditions I was actually working in. Every honor roll semester, every NTHS meeting I ran as Secretary, every application essay I drafted on my own, those were answers to a challenge that had no official solution.

I am heading to North Carolina A&T State University to study Mechanical Engineering. I am going as someone who built the path there from scratch. And I am going prepared, because being first-generation did not break me. It made me thorough.
