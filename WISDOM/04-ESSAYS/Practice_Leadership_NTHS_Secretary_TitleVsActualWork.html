The Work Behind the Title

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC
Prompt: Describe your leadership experience.

When I was elected Secretary of the National Technical Honor Society, I inherited a role. A title, a set of responsibilities, a folder of past meeting minutes. What I chose to do with it was entirely up to me. And I think that distinction matters more than people realize, because leadership is not the position. Leadership is what happens between the meetings.

The Secretary role is easy to underestimate. On the surface you take notes. But in practice, the Secretary is the organizational backbone of the whole group. I keep the agenda clear before every meeting so officers are not wasting time figuring out what we are even doing. I record the minutes with enough detail that someone who was absent can read them and actually know what was decided, not just what was discussed. I track action items so that "we should do this" becomes "this got done." Over three years I built a system that the organization can rely on regardless of who else shows up or what else is going on.

What I did not expect was how much of the role would be about people, not paper. When two officers disagreed about how to structure an event, I was usually the one who drafted the proposal that bridged the gap between them. When members were disengaged, I checked in individually rather than making it a whole group conversation. I learned that most of the real work in any organization happens before the meeting starts, not during it. Leadership there is mostly relational. It is knowing when to speak and when to handle something quietly.

Code Forward gave me a completely different kind of leadership. As a camp instructor, I was in front of a group of students who had zero obligation to engage with me. They were there for the summer. Whether they actually learned anything came down to whether I could earn their attention and hold it. I could not rely on authority. I had to rely on clarity, energy, and making the material feel relevant. I taught HTML and CSS by helping students build something they actually wanted, a page about themselves, a portfolio, something they cared about. And I learned that teaching is not about knowing the material. It is about knowing which explanation will land for a specific person in a specific moment.

The common thread in both is that leadership is a service. The Secretary serves the organization's need for structure and follow-through. The instructor serves the student's need to feel capable and make progress. I did not run for NTHS Secretary to put something on a resume. I ran because I am genuinely good at structure and I knew the organization needed someone who would actually do the work the role requires. That is the kind of leader I am trying to be. Useful, consistent, and accountable to the people I am supposed to be helping.
