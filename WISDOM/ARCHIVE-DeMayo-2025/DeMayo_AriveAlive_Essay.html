The Message Nobody Sends

Wisdom M. Johnson | Pine Forest High School | Fayetteville, NC

Nobody gets in their car planning to be the last text message someone ever receives. That is the part that makes distracted and impaired driving so hard to talk about — it does not feel dangerous in the moment. It feels like a quick glance. It feels like one more drink before leaving. It feels manageable, right up until it is not.

I grew up in Fayetteville, a military city where people come and go quickly and where the roads stay busy at all hours. Before I ever had a driver's license, I had already sat in a car with someone who was texting at a red light, watched a driver drift across the center line at night, and heard stories from friends about close calls that never made it into any report. Distracted and impaired driving was not an abstract statistic to me. It was part of the landscape.

What I have come to understand is that awareness alone does not save lives. Most people already know that texting while driving is dangerous. Most people already know not to drink and drive. The problem is not information — it is decision-making in the moment when consequences feel far away and the phone buzzes or the keys are already in your hand. The gap between knowing and doing is where people get killed.

That gap is something I think about as someone entering engineering. Engineers do not just identify problems — they design systems that make the right choice easier than the wrong one. Seatbelts work not because people decided to value their lives more, but because the car was designed to remind them. Modern vehicles now have forward collision warnings, lane departure alerts, and ignition interlocks. These are not coincidences. They are the result of engineers who understood that the most effective intervention happens before the mistake, not after.

My generation sits at a unique intersection of this problem. We are the most connected generation in history, which means we are also the most distracted. Smartphones have made it harder to be present anywhere — at dinner, in class, and especially behind the wheel. I have watched friends respond to notifications at stoplights without thinking twice about it, the same way someone might tap their foot to a song. It has become reflex. Breaking a reflex requires more than a poster on a wall. It requires redesigning the environment.

This is why I believe the most powerful thing young people can do is not just change their own behavior, but influence the systems and technology around them. As a student in the Academy of Information Technology and an incoming engineering student at North Carolina A&T, I intend to work on exactly that kind of problem — building tools and systems that help people make better decisions without relying entirely on willpower. Whether that means designing smarter vehicle interfaces, developing apps that disable notifications while driving, or contributing to autonomous safety systems, I want to be part of the generation that engineers solutions rather than simply campaigns.

In the meantime, the most honest thing I can do is start with myself and the people around me. I do not pick up my phone when I am in a car, even as a passenger, because I refuse to model the behavior I am asking others to avoid. When I am with friends, I say something. It is awkward exactly once, and then it is not. Silence is not neutrality — silence is permission.

The message nobody sends is the one that could have waited. Every text, every notification, every glance away from the road is a bet that nothing will happen in those two seconds. Most of the time, the bet pays off. But the times it does not are measured in funerals, not fender benders. I want to be part of a generation that stops making that bet — and starts building roads, cars, and communities where arriving alive is not luck. It is the design.
