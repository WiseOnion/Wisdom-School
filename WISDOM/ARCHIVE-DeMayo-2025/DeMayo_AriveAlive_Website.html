Why It Matters

The Gap Between Knowing and Doing

Most people already know distracted and drunk driving is dangerous. The problem isn't information, it's the split-second decision when the phone buzzes or the keys are already in your hand.

"Nobody gets in their car planning to be the last text message someone ever receives. Silence isn't neutrality. Silence is permission. Say something." Wisdom Johnson, Pine Forest High School
Make the Pledge
Make a Commitment

The Arrive Alive Pledge

It takes less than a minute to commit to something that could save your life or someone else's.

1
Put the phone away
Before you start the car, place your phone in your bag, glove box, or backseat. Out of sight means out of temptation.
2
Plan ahead
If you're drinking, arrange a ride before you start. Designate a driver. Use Uber or Lyft. There is always a better option.
3
Speak up
If you're in a car with a distracted or impaired driver, say something. It's awkward exactly once. A funeral is forever.
4
Share the message
Tell a friend. Post it. The more people who commit, the more lives we save. Change starts with one conversation.

Stay Safe

Practical Ways to Protect Yourself

Small habits make a massive difference every time you get behind the wheel.

Enable Do Not Disturb While Driving
Both iPhone and Android have built-in driving modes that silence notifications automatically when in motion. Turn it on and leave it on.
Set Your Music Before You Move
Queue your playlist or navigation before you put the car in drive. Adjusting settings while moving is just as dangerous as texting.
Rideshare Is Always an Option
There is no situation where a $15 Uber isn't worth it. Program the app before you go out, not after you've already been drinking.
Be the Friend Who Says Something
If someone is about to drive impaired or distracted, take the keys. Offer to drive. Call a ride. Real friends don't stay silent.
That Text Can Wait
Whatever that message says, it will still be there when you park. No notification, no reply, no update is worth your life.
Know Your Limits, Then Cut Them in Half
Impairment starts before you feel drunk. Reaction time slows and overconfidence rises, often before you notice it happening.
